1 Equations/Summaries

The general ones.

  • Current, \(I\), refers to rate of flow of electric charge
    • Measured in amperes (A, amps, \(1A = 1 C/s\))
    • Also, \(I = \frac{Q}{t}\)
  • Resistance, \(R\)
    • Measured in ohms (Ω - Greek capital omega, \(1Ω = 1V/A\))
    • For metals \(R\) is constant (disregarding temperature) and independent of voltage (it’s an inherent property of the material)
      • Thicker wire = less resistance
  • Voltage, \(V=IR\)
    • Voltage, aka electric potential difference (in Volts)
    • Not potential energy (\(\Delta PE\)) but potential energy per unit charge
  • Power, \(P=IV\)
    • Power (in Watts, 1W=1 J/s)
    • For resistors:
      • Voltage and Resistance: \(P=\frac{V^2}{R}\)
      • Current and Resistance: \(P=I^2R\)
  • Ohm’s Law:
    • Current is proportional to potential difference - \(I \propto V\)
  • In Series: (picture)
    • When in series (end to end in a single linear path), resistors sum to form larger resistor
    • \(V=V_1 + V_2 + V_3 = IR_1 + IR_2 + IR_3\)
      • \(V = I \cdot R_{EQ}\)
    • \(R_{EQ} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3\)… - equivalent resistance (combining the resistors to simplify)
  • In Parallel: (picture)
    • When in parallel the net resistance is less than any of the individual resistances
    • \(\frac{1}{R_{EQ}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3}\)
  • Kirchhoff’s Rules
    1. Current Law aka Junction Rule (KCL): the sum of all currents entering the junction equals the sum of all currents leaving that junction
      • \(\Sigma i_n = 0\), or \(I_{in} = I_{out}\)
      • Junction - where three or more wires meet
    2. Voltage Law aka Loop Rule (KVL): conservation of energy, the sum of the changes in potential in a circuit must be zero
      • \(\Sigma V_{loop} = 0\)
      • Battery (or any EMF - ε) in the loop:
Loop - to + Voltage +
Loop + to - Voltage -
  • Measurement Devices
    • Ammeter - measures amps, wire in series
    • Voltmeter - measures volts, wire in parallel
    • Multimeter - does all of them
  • Batteries
    • In series, voltage is sum
    • In parallel, voltage is same but increased current

4 Unit Topics/Objectives

Very basic ones have omitted deleted to keep list shorter.

4.1 Theoretical

4.1.1 Lesser Difficulty

  • Understand that all charges move simultaneously in a closed circuit.
    • All the electrons are already in the circuit, the potential difference makes them move
  • Understand that compass deflection indicates charge flow in one direction.
  • Understand that battery orientation determines the direction of charge flow.
    • See chart below
  • Understand that charge is conserved in a circuit.
    • “Circuits don’t create, destroy, use up, or lose electrons. They just carry the electrons around in circles.”
  • Understand that the battery is the pump and not the supplier of charge.
    • All the electrons are already in the circuit, the potential difference makes them move
    • “The pump creates a voltage difference along the circuit which drives charges like electrons through the circuit.”
  • Understand the charges that flow in circuits are electrons and they flow opposite to the direction of conventional flow.
Conventional current + to -
Electron flow - to +
  • Understand how ammeters and voltmeters must be connected in a circuit.
    • Ammeter - series, voltmeter parallel

4.1.2 Higher Difficulty

  • Be able to determine the conducting path of a light bulb.
  • Understand that the filaments control the rate of charge flow
    • Less resistance = more current flow
  • Understand that adding bulbs in series decreases flow rate whereas adding bulbs in parallel increases flow rate
    • Less resistance = more current flow. Series resistors = more resistance, parallel resistors = less resistance.
  • Understand that resistance is an object’s ability to decrease flow rate.
    • Picture
  • Understand that voltage drop is an electrical potential difference between two points.
  • Understand that motion of charge is caused by pressure or potential differences (voltage drop).
    • Think: rollercoaster analogy, high points and low points. Or waterfall analogy.
  • Understand the difference between AC and DC and know which way circuits are constructed in our homes. (Jake note - I think he forgot to cross this out but idk)

4.2 Skills

4.2.1 Lesser Difficulty

  • Draw the schematic diagram for a circuit which consists of a battery, bulb, and a capacitor.
  • Be able to determine the conducting path of a light bulb.
  • Know the symbols and units for charge, current, resistance, voltage, power and use the definition of Ohm’s Law and the Power equations to solve math problems.

4.2.2 Higher Difficulty

  • Know how to find equivalent resistance of resistors in series and in parallel
  • Understand how to simplify circuits and find current through and voltage across resistors.
  • Understand how to use Kirchoff’s rules to find current through and voltage across resistors.
