2 The Gilded Age

Monday, March 01, 2021

“In United States history, the Gilded Age was an era that occurred during the late 19th century, from the 1870s to about 1900. The Gilded Age was an era of rapid economic growth, especially in the Northern and Western United States.”

How did it change our country?

2.1 Pre-Gilded Age

  • Not much of a middle class
    • Most people are either lower class or exceptionally rich
  • Lots of stuff being invented

2.2 Post Gilded Age

  • Expanded middle class
  • Higher incomes
  • Increased leisure time
  • Primed for consumer culture
  • Better education, including for women

3 Labor Unions

Really got going after Civil War. Because not enough workers, they can start making some demands

3.1 Goals of Labor Unions

  1. Wages
  2. Hours
  3. Working conditions
  4. Child labor

3.2 Early Labor Unions

  • National Labor Union
    • Didn’t do so well.
  • Knights of Labor
    • Thinking more about small business than wage labor.
  • American Federation of Labor (AFL)
    • Founded by Samuel Gompers
    • Different philosophy of how to organize people as workers
      • Bunch of small unions rather than a huge worker’s union

3.3 Tools of Labor Unions

3.3.1 Collective Bargaining

  • Group of people asking for something
  • One person - business doesn’t have to listen
  • Hundreds - they do

3.3.2 Strikes

When collective bargaining breaks down

3.3.3 Boycotts

3.3.4 Demonstrations - work stoppages

3.4 Major Labor Strikes

3.4.1 Great Railway Strike of 1877

3.4.2 Haymarket Bombing

  • Seen as symbol of anarchism
  • Gave negative light to the work and goals of labor organizations

3.4.3 Homestead Strike

  • Violently beaten down and out of existence

3.4.4 Pullman Strike

  • Chicago
  • People in Pullman, Chicago worked for Pullman Company making luxury rail cars
  • Workers lived in Pullman Village
    • Had to rent at Pullman Village
    • Had to shop at Pullman stores
  • Company controlled housing, food, furniture
  1. Workers couldn’t take it anymore
    • Strike/uprising
    • Violently crushed

3.4.5 Problems With Strikes

  • Turned public opinion against workers
  • Violent/“anarchist”
  • Radical
    • People don’t like radical
    • Bolshevik Revolution - 1917

3.5 General Union Notes

  • Some states make it hard for unions to organize
  • Union workers do better
    • Better pay, better hours, better everything

3.5.1 Unions vs Top 1%

  • Reagan - anti-Union
  • More union - 1% has less wealth
  • Less union - 1% has more wealth

3.6 National Labor Relations Act

4 Populism

Monday, March 08, 2021

Populism - a political approach that strives to appeal to ordinary people who feel that their concerns are disregarded by established elite groups

4.1 Omaha Platform

  • Statement by Populist Party (their platform)
  • Highly radical
  • Many people doubted they could acheive what was on the platform (too radical)

4.1.1 Sub-treasuries

  • Government would buy up crops/resources and slowly sell them
  • Prevented an increase in supply but stable demand (prices drop in this case)

4.2 Farmers Discontent in the 1890s

  • Debt
  • Not enough farmer representation
  • Deflation (paying more for purchases than they recieve for their products)
    • Prices drop
  • Farmers

4.2.1 Natural Debtors

Farmers are natural debtors

  • Beginning of growing season, take out loans:
    • Seeds
    • Fertilizers
    • Materials
  1. Example
    • Deflation of 10%
      • $100 loan
      • Sell crops for $135
      • Pay off debts $100
      • Profit $25
    • Inflation of 10%
      • $100 loan
      • Sell crops for $165
      • Pay off debts $110
      • Profit $55

4.3 Metals

4.3.1 Goldbugs

  • Bankers and businessmen
  • Wanted gold standard
    • Tight money (less $ in circulation)
  • Why: loans repaid in stable money
  • Deffects
    • Deflation
    • Prices fall

4.3.2 Silverites (bimetallism)

  • Wanted gold & silver
    • More $ in circulation
  • Less stable

4.4 Election of 1896

  • Victory of Republican McKinley
  • RIP Populists
  • Big deal over bi-metalism
  • Adding silver to the gold standard
  • More people less gold would mean deflation
  • More money in circulation = onflation

4.5 Populists of 1890s vs Populists Today

4.5.1 Religion

  • Religious imagery used heavily

4.5.2 Anti-Immigrant

  • Associated with:
    • Disease
    • Poverty
  • Most immigrants in 1890s were Eastern European
    • Different religion — Catholic or Jewish
  • Now - “Mexicans are rapists”

4.5.3 Anti-Elite

  • Now - “Drain the swamp”

4.5.4 Agrarian Interests

  • Populists grew out of Granger Movement
  • Ultimately became Populist Party
  • Now - Trump appeal to farmers

Republican

      • Conservation
        • Expanded National Parks system
        • Put more land under federal control

5.1.2 William Howard Taft

Republican

  • “Designated successor” for Roosevelt

5.1.3 Woodrow Wilson

Democrat, from Virginia

5.2 Election of 1912

  • Roosevelt wanted to run again, wanted Republican nomination but they wouldn’t give it
  • Roosevelt founded new party
    • Progressive/populist, called Bull Moose
  • Split Republican vote between Roosevelt and Taft, opened door to Democrats (Wilson) to win
  • Fun thing, socialist got ~7% of vote (Debs)

5.3 Wilson’s New Freedom

5.3.1 Tarrif Reform

Underwood Simmons Tariff

  • Lowered tarriff for first time in 65 years
    • Important for hurting big business (?)

5.3.2 Trust Freedom

Clayton Anti-Trust Act

  • Very effective at breaking up the monopolies
    • More so than any act before then

5.3.3 The Bank - Federal Reserve System

  • Two functions that can “push levers of economy”
    • Control how much money is in circulation
      • They give money to banks
      • Can send money to places in need (pull money from one place send to other)
    • Set interest rates
      • If economy is slowing down they can lower interest rate so more people can/will take out loans
      • If economy is going too high they can raise rataes to pull economy down

7.6 Keynesian Economics

Method for when you have inflation/deflation

  • During Great Depression there was deflation
  • Method to solve deflation (prices down):
    • Put more money into circulation
      • Get hands on more gold so you can print more money, or lower ratio of gold to dollar
    • Taxes down
    • Increase spending
      • Public works and workers, for example
    • better public image
      • Treating deflation would make you more popular
  • Method to solve inflation (prices up):
    • Pull money away from people and put it into treasury (using taxes)
    • Taxes up
    • Decrease spending
    • worse public image
      • Treating inflation would make you unpopular
      • In America since 1945 - we don’t treat inflation (because it’s so unpopular) (between 3% and 5% each year)

11 1950s

Why did people have so much money in the 1950s? - greatest economic expansion we had ever experienced

11.1 Pre-50s - the setup (WWII)

  • WWII there was rationing, you couldn’t really buy stuff
    • No movie theaters, no car production, etc
  • Therefore everyone was working during the war and saving all that money
  • Once the war ended everyone went out and spent a ton

11.2 Baby Boom

  • Births between 1946 and 1960 went up a lot
  • Big group of people doing stuff around the same time
    • School, college, etc
  • Caused housing shortage
    • Solved by Levittowns (went up fast and easy)
  • Always been a very big group
  • Fun issue: Baby Boomers will cause/do cause issue on SSA because they’re so big

11.3 Great Society

  • Wage war against poverty
    • Even though middle class was doing well there was still a huge swath of people who were not
  • LBJ
  • Great inequality
  • Stuff done by LBJ at the time stayed, they weren’t temporary measures

11.3.1 Reforms & Acts

Expanded the government’s role. However, many Republicans thought it was way too much money being spent by the government which means higher taxes.

  • Voting Rights Act 1965
  • Immigration Act of 1965
  • Job Corps and VISTA
  • Food Stamp Act of 1964
  • Social Security Reforms
    • Anti-discriminatory
  • Elementary and Secondary Education Act
    • Food in schools
  • Medicare
    • Anyone over age of 65
    • Works same way as SSA - employee & employer pay into it
  • Medicaid
    • Based on income
    • Below certain level government pays for medical care
    • Comes from taxpayer dollars
  • Welfare

11.3.2 Problems

  • LBJ was throwing all money into Vietnam war so there really wasn’t enough for domestic use
    • Armaments, troop pay, etc
